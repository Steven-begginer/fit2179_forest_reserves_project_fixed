The Changing Map of Malaysia's Forest Reserves

A story-driven visualisation about how Malaysia's forest reserve areas changed from 2003 to 2021, why state-level differences matter, and how these patterns connect with population and rainfall/flood context.

Why this visualisation?

National totals can hide regional differences. This webpage uses maps and diagrams to show which states hold the largest forest reserves, which states gained or lost protected forest area, and why these changes deserve public attention.

01

National overview

  • Peaked in 2009 at 13.12 M ha
  • Fell sharply to a low of 12.32 M ha in 2019
  • A net loss of ~0.8 M ha over the decade
02

Forest coverage across Malaysian states (2021)

  • Sabah leads with 48.5% of its state land under forest reserve, followed by Perak (46.8%) and Pahang (44.1%) — all topping 40%
  • Urban and small states lag far behind — Melaka (3.0%), Pulau Pinang (5.0%), and W.P. Kuala Lumpur (0.3%) have almost no forest reserve relative to their land area
03

Forest reserve composition over time

  • Sarawak's decline drove national losses - shrank from 4.84 M ha to 3.96 M ha (2003-2021), accounting for nearly all of Malaysia's total reduction.
  • As Sarawak shrank, Sabah's share rose - Sabah's band now nearly equals Sarawak's, reflecting a gradual shift in which state dominates the total.
04

Which states gained or lost forest?

  • Kelantan held steady - virtually unchanged at 0.0% over 2003-2021.
  • Sarawak dropped sharply - down 18.0%, the largest loss of any state by far.
  • Perak and W.P. Kuala Lumpur gained the most - up +11.6% and +11.5% respectively.
05

Forest reserve per person

  • Sarawak towers above all - 1,408 ha per 1,000 people, over 1.5x the next highest (Pahang 946, Sabah 914).
  • Urban states have near zero - Selangor (38), Melaka (6), Pulau Pinang (3), and W.P. KL (0.04) reflect dense populations with virtually no reserve per person.
06

Forest share vs population share

  • Sarawak is over-forested relative to its people - holds ~32% of national forest reserves but only ~8.6% of population.
  • Selangor is the starkest mismatch - home to ~20% of Malaysia's population but just ~2% of its forest reserves.
07

Flood rate heatmap

  • Pahang, Kedah, Negeri Sembilan, and Terengganu faced repeated heavy flooding - each shows multiple dark-orange years across 2003-2010.
  • 2006-2009 was the broadest high-flood period - many states intensified at the same time, showing that severe flooding was widespread rather than isolated.
08

District flood frequency map

  • Pahang is the clearest hotspot - Jerantut, Maran, Pekan, and Temerloh each recorded flooding in 7 of 8 years, showing repeated district-level exposure within one state.

Data note: District point locations are estimated from the average coordinates of towns in each district. Dot size counts how many years each district recorded flooding between 2003 and 2010.

09

Pahang flood persistence vs tree cover

  • High tree cover did not prevent repeated flooding - Jerantut, Pekan, and Temerloh all have about 89-97% tree cover, yet each still flooded in 7 of 8 years.
  • Kuantan is the main contrast case - it has the lowest tree-cover share at about 83% and fewer flood years (3 of 8), so the chart does not show a simple "less tree cover = more flooding" pattern.
10

National forest cover vs forest reserve area

  • Both forest cover and reserve area declined together.
  • The gap between them narrowed over time.

Data note: Forest reserve area is from Malaysia’s official gazetted records; national forest cover is from the global deforestation dataset. Both are indexed to 2003 = 100 for direct comparison.

Key takeaway

Malaysia's forest reserve story is uneven. A national trend is useful, but the state-level view reveals the real geography of forest protection: where reserve areas are concentrated, where they changed most, and how these patterns relate to people and environmental pressure.
